Why LSA Fits Locksmiths Better Than Most Trades

Most home service businesses benefit from LSA because it puts them at the top of Google with a verified badge and a direct call button. Locksmiths benefit from all of that and then some, because the locksmith search experience has been uniquely terrible for consumers for over a decade.

Here is the core problem: when someone searches "locksmith near me," Google organic results and even Google Ads results can be gamed. Fake operators have gotten very good at appearing local. They pay for ads, they build keyword-stuffed websites, they register addresses that turn out to be UPS stores or vacant lots. A homeowner who does not know what to look for has no reliable way to tell the legitimate businesses from the scammers just by reading search results.

LSA changes that equation in three specific ways that matter to locksmiths:

  • Your address is verified. Google confirms you operate from a real, physical location in your claimed service area. Fake operators running out of call centers in other states cannot pass this check.
  • Background checks are mandatory. Every owner and employee who enters customer homes goes through a background screening. This is a layer of consumer protection that no regular Google ad provides.
  • Your license is on file. In states that require locksmith licensing, Google confirms you hold a current, valid license. Unlicensed operators cannot fake their way through this step.

The result is that your LSA listing signals something a 9-locksmith Google Ad cannot: this person is who they say they are. For a customer who has heard the horror stories, that signal is worth a lot.

The emergency nature of locksmith work is also a structural advantage. Lockouts happen at 11pm during a snowstorm, in hospital parking lots, on a Tuesday afternoon when someone locked their keys in their car outside a grocery store. The person searching is not comparison shopping. They are pressing the first trustworthy call button they see. LSA puts your call button at the very top of that search, above everything else on the page.

Response time is more important here than in any other trade. A homeowner locked out at 10pm is not going to wait patiently for a callback. If you do not answer within two or three minutes, they are dialing the next listing. Your answer rate, not just your ad position, is a core part of your competitive performance on LSA.

Locksmith Licensing Requirements by State

More than 22 states require some form of locksmith licensing. This is more regulated than painting but less regulated than electrical or HVAC. Google checks your state requirements during LSA verification, so knowing what applies to you before you start is essential. Submitting the wrong documents or leaving the license field blank is the single most common reason locksmiths get stuck in verification for weeks.

If you are in a state without a locksmith license requirement, do not leave the field blank during LSA verification. Note clearly that your state has no licensing requirement for locksmiths. This reduces the chance of triggering a manual review request that drags out your verification timeline.

If you are in a state that does require a license and you do not have one, you need to get licensed before you can complete LSA verification. There is no workaround. Getting this handled before you start the application is better than getting partway through verification and discovering the problem.

Which Job Types to Enable (and the Smart Lock Opportunity)

Most locksmiths enable the obvious categories: residential lockout, commercial lockout, automotive lockout. That is a fine start and covers the emergency bread-and-butter work. But leaving it there means you are invisible for a significant chunk of searches happening in your market every month.

Here is the full list of job types to consider enabling on your locksmith LSA profile:

  • Residential lockout. The core emergency category. Enable this, obviously.
  • Commercial lockout. Business owners locked out of their office or retail space, often before or after normal business hours. Higher urgency, often higher job value.
  • Automotive/car lockout. Keys locked in the car. High-volume and fast to resolve. Confirm this is a separate category in your market, as availability varies by region.
  • Rekeying locks. Homeowners who just moved in, recent breakups, lost key situations, or landlords after an eviction. Steady, non-emergency demand that runs year-round.
  • Lock installation. New construction, upgrades, or replacements after damage. Pairs well with rekeying and lock repair in the same profile.
  • Lock repair. Damaged or malfunctioning locks that need service rather than replacement. Steady volume in residential and commercial markets.
  • Key duplication. Available in some markets as a separate LSA job type. Quick jobs with decent volume in residential areas.
  • Safe unlocking. Higher-value jobs. A homeowner who inherited a safe or forgot a combination is not calling five locksmiths to compare prices. If you offer this, enable it.

The Smart Lock Opportunity Most Locksmiths Are Missing

Here is the category most locksmiths have not thought about yet: smart lock installation.

Sales of smart locks have grown significantly over the past several years. Products like Nest x Yale, August Smart Lock, and Schlage Encode are in millions of homes and being added to millions more. The problem is that most homeowners have no idea that installing a smart lock correctly is not as simple as YouTube makes it look. Alignment, strike plate adjustments, deadbolt calibration, and pairing with existing security systems can all cause problems. And when the lock malfunctions at 11pm because the installation was not done right, they are not calling the person who sold them the lock. They are calling a locksmith.

If you offer smart lock installation, enable that job type in your LSA profile. The vast majority of locksmiths have not done this yet, which means the competition per search in this category is extremely low. It is a legitimate service, it is a growing category, and you are probably looking at a market where you are one of a handful of verified locksmiths who even show up for those searches.

Setting Your Hours for Maximum Emergency Lead Volume

This section is more important for locksmiths than it is for almost any other trade, and most locksmiths do not think about it carefully enough.

Lockout calls happen at all hours. A homeowner locked out at 11pm. A business owner who cannot get into their office at 6:30am before staff arrive. A driver locked out in a grocery store parking lot on a Sunday afternoon. These are real call patterns, and they represent calls that go to exactly one type of locksmith: the one who is showing as available when the search happens.

If your LSA hours are set to 8am to 6pm Monday through Friday, you are invisible to every person who searches outside those hours. If a competitor across town has set their hours to 7am to 11pm or even 24/7, they are capturing every one of those calls by default. No competitive bidding needed, no better reviews required. They just showed up.

Here is how to approach your hours settings:

  • If you genuinely offer 24/7 service, set 24/7. You will capture calls that have zero competition from the majority of your market. A 2am lockout call has almost no other verified locksmiths in the results. The call volume at off-hours may be lower in absolute terms, but your conversion rate is sky-high because the caller has immediate need and no real alternative.
  • If you offer extended hours but not 24/7, set your actual hours accurately. Something like 7am to 11pm, seven days a week, covers the vast majority of emergency lockout situations while being realistic about your capacity.
  • Emergency-only after-hours? Some locksmiths take emergency calls after a certain hour but not routine jobs. If that is you, reflect it in your hours and service type settings.
  • Never set hours you cannot honor. Listing 24/7 hours, getting a call at 1am, and not picking up hurts your response rate in a way that takes time to recover from. Better to have accurate, narrower hours and actually answer every call within them.
Know your numbers before you set your budget.

Review Strategy for a Scam-Wary Industry

In most home service trades, a five-star review that says "great service, fast response, highly recommend" is perfectly fine. In the locksmith trade, those reviews land differently because potential customers reading them have been primed to wonder if the business is legitimate. A generic positive review does not address the specific fear driving the search.

The fear driving a locksmith search is this: "Is this person going to quote me 9 and charge me 00?" That is the actual question running in the background when someone reads your reviews. Generic positive reviews do not answer it. Specific reviews that mention pricing directly do.

This means your review request strategy needs to be more intentional than what works in other trades.

What to Ask for When Requesting Reviews

Do not just send a link to your Google Business Profile and ask for "a review." Give customers context on what to include. Something like: "If you are happy with the work, would you mind leaving a review mentioning the price you were quoted and what it ended up being? A lot of people searching for locksmiths worry about hidden charges, and a specific note about your experience with our pricing helps new customers feel safe calling us."

That framing costs you nothing. And a review that says "quoted me 5 for a residential lockout, that is exactly what I paid, showed up in 18 minutes" does ten times more work than "fast and professional!"

Review Benchmarks to Hit

  • Under 20 reviews: You are ranked primarily on proximity and profile completeness. Focus entirely on getting reviews before anything else.
  • 20 to 50 reviews: You start competing on ranking. Review recency matters here. One review a week beats three reviews in one week followed by silence for two months.
  • 50+ reviews: You are in the top tier for most suburban markets. At this point, rating quality and review content start differentiating you from competitors with similar counts.
  • 4.8+ star average: The threshold where Google algorithm gives you a measurable ranking boost. Getting from 4.6 to 4.8 is worth prioritizing through proactive review requests and careful handling of negative reviews.

Handling Negative Reviews in the Locksmith Trade

Negative reviews in any trade are uncomfortable. In the locksmith trade, a negative review that mentions pricing or deceptive practices is an existential threat to your listing, because every potential customer reading it is already primed to believe the worst.

Respond to every negative review. Respond fast. And respond specifically. Do not paste a generic "we are sorry you feel this way, please contact us." Address the specific concern. If the pricing complaint is unfounded, explain your rate structure calmly. If something genuinely went wrong, acknowledge it, explain what you have done to address it, and offer a path to resolution.

A locksmith who responds professionally to a negative review actually builds more trust than one who has only pristine reviews. It shows real customers, real problems handled like a real business. That is the antidote to the fake operator reputation that hangs over the whole industry.

Common Mistakes Locksmiths Make on LSA

  • Not enabling smart lock installation. This category has virtually no competition in most markets because most locksmiths have not turned it on. If you offer smart lock installation, enable it today.
  • Setting hours that are too narrow. If your LSA hours stop at 6pm and emergency lockout calls peak between 8pm and midnight, you are invisible during your highest-intent search window. Set hours to match your actual availability, even if that just means extending to 10pm or 11pm to start.
  • Not asking for reviews that mention pricing. Generic five-star reviews do not counter the scam reputation that every potential customer brings to a locksmith search. Ask customers specifically to mention their quote and final price in the review. Those details move the needle.
  • Missing automotive lockout as a job type. Car lockouts are high-volume and fast to resolve. If you do them but have not enabled this in your LSA profile, you are giving those leads to competitors who did.
  • Ignoring the safe unlocking category. These are low-volume but high-value jobs, often 00 to 00+, and callers are not price-shopping. If you offer safe unlocking, enable it. Most locksmiths in your market probably have not.
  • Setting a service area too large to actually cover. Locksmiths live and die on response time. A customer locked out at 11pm who hears "we can be there in 75 minutes" is hanging up and calling the next listing. Set a service area you can realistically reach in 20 to 40 minutes for emergency calls.
  • Not quoting a price on the first call. The moment you give a price range before anyone gets in a van, you remove the primary fear that makes customers hang up on locksmiths. "Our service call is and a standard residential lockout is , that is what you will pay unless there is something unusual with the lock and we will tell you before we touch it." That sentence closes more calls than any amount of advertising optimization.
