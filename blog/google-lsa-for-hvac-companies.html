TL;DR — Is LSA Worth It for HVAC?

Yes — especially during peak season. HVAC LSA leads come in ready to book. You're not paying for someone who's just browsing or comparing prices six months out. You're paying for the person whose furnace quit on a cold Tuesday night and needs someone today.

That said, LSA alone won't fill your schedule year-round. The best HVAC companies run LSA for immediate high-intent leads and layer Google Search Ads on top for broader coverage, maintenance plan signups, and off-peak demand. More on that below.

Typical HVAC LSA leads cost $35–$70 in most mid-size U.S. markets. At a 50% booking rate, that's roughly $70–$140 per booked appointment. On a $200 tune-up that margin is tight — but on a $5,000 AC replacement, the return is exceptional. LSA economics are strongest when you're capturing your highest-value job types, which is exactly what a well-optimized profile is built to surface.

What Google LSA Actually Looks Like for HVAC

When someone searches "AC repair near me" or "furnace not working," Google surfaces a row of Local Services Ads at the very top of the page — above regular Google Ads and above organic results. Each listing shows your business name, star rating, number of reviews, years in business, and a click-to-call button.

For HVAC, this placement is gold. Emergency searches convert almost instantly. When homeowners in your area search for urgent AC or heating help, they're not browsing — they're hot, uncomfortable, and ready to book whoever answers the phone. Your job is to be in that top pack and pick up when they call.

The "Google Guaranteed" badge (now called "Google Verified") that appears on your listing tells homeowners that Google has checked your licenses, insurance, and background. That badge alone sets you apart from the unverified contractors showing up in organic results below.

How to Set Up Google LSA for Your HVAC Company

Getting set up takes a bit of work upfront, but once you're live it's one of the lowest-maintenance lead sources you'll run. Here's the step-by-step:

  1. Create your LSA profile at ads.google.com/local-services-ads — add your business name, service areas, hours, and a description that clearly explains what you do and who you serve.
  2. Select your HVAC job types — this is critical. Enable every service you actually offer: AC repair, AC installation, heating repair, furnace installation, heat pump service, duct cleaning, thermostat installation, air quality services. If it's not enabled, Google won't show your ad for it.
  3. Upload your license and insurance documents — Google requires proof of your HVAC contractor license and general liability insurance. Have digital copies ready to upload.
  4. Complete background checks — owners typically need a background check. Some markets require technicians too. This is the slowest step, so start it first.
  5. Connect your Google Business Profile — your GBP reviews feed directly into your LSA listing. Make sure your GBP is complete, accurate, and actively collecting reviews before you go live.
  6. Set your budget and bid mode — start with Maximize Leads and a weekly budget that matches roughly 10 to 15 leads per week at your expected cost per lead. You can always adjust once you see real data.
  7. Add photos and complete your profile — real photos of your team, trucks, and completed jobs. No stock photos. Google rewards complete profiles with better placement and lower CPL.

How to Rank Higher in HVAC Local Services Ads

LSA runs on an auction. Google doesn't just show the highest bidder — it weighs your bid against your profile quality and how likely a customer is to actually contact you. Here's what moves the needle for HVAC specifically:

  • Manage your review velocity around seasonal swings. HVAC demand peaks hard in summer and winter, which means your reviews naturally cluster during those months. The problem is Google's algorithm notices when reviews go dead in April and October. Build a system that collects reviews year-round — even on maintenance calls and tune-ups during shoulder season. A steady 8–10 reviews per month beats 40 in July and zero in March.
  • Staff your phones for 2am furnace calls. HVAC is one of the few trades where emergency calls come in at all hours. A homeowner with no heat at 2am in January is calling whoever answers first. Google tracks your answer rate and penalizes missed calls, but beyond ranking — that after-hours call is often a $4,000+ system replacement. Use an answering service or on-call rotation so you never send an emergency caller to voicemail.
  • Enable every HVAC-specific job type — especially the ones you forgot. Most HVAC contractors enable AC repair and furnace installation but skip mini-split service, heat pump repair, duct cleaning, indoor air quality, and thermostat installation. Every unchecked box is a search you're invisible for. Go through the full list today and turn on everything you can actually deliver.
  • Maintain review momentum through the off-season. Your competitors are pulling back on marketing during spring and fall. That's your window to build ranking at lower cost. Keep a baseline LSA budget running, collect reviews on every maintenance and tune-up call, and add fresh job photos. The ranking ground you gain in March pays off when July demand hits and every HVAC company in town is competing for the same leads.
  • Use Maximize Leads — don't cap yourself during peak season. Setting a manual max per lead limit of $45 in a market where summer AC leads cost $65–$80 means your ads barely show during the weeks that matter most. Switch to Maximize Leads and set your weekly budget to a number you can sustain through your busiest month. Let Google find the leads; you focus on answering and booking.

HVAC Seasonality and LSA — How to Play It Smart

HVAC demand is the most seasonal of any home service category. You have two peak windows — summer AC season and winter heating season — with slower shoulder periods in between. Your LSA strategy should reflect that.

During peak season: Increase your weekly budget before demand spikes, not after. If you wait until July to turn up your LSA spend, you've already missed the first wave. Ramp up in May for AC season and in October for heating season. Make sure your phones are staffed and your team can handle the volume. Customers searching near you during a heat wave aren't going to wait — they're calling whoever answers.

During shoulder seasons: Don't turn LSA off — keep a lower budget running for maintenance calls and installs. This is also the best time to focus on reviews. Customers are less stressed, more likely to respond positively, and your competitors may be pulling back spend. That's an opportunity to gain ground in the rankings at lower cost.

Off-season moves that pay off in-season: Use slower months to build your review count, add photos, complete any profile gaps, and make sure your Google Business Profile is dialed in. The ranking work you do in the off-season shows up as better placement when demand peaks.

Common LSA Mistakes HVAC Companies Make

  • Not adjusting budget for seasonal demand. HVAC companies that set a flat $500/week budget all year burn through it in three days during a July heat wave and have nothing left for the rest of the week. Build a seasonal budget plan: $800–$1,200/week during summer AC season and winter heating peaks, $300–$500/week during spring and fall shoulder months. Match your spend to when homeowners actually need you.
  • Only enabling the obvious services. AC repair and furnace installation are checked on every HVAC profile. But duct cleaning, indoor air quality testing, mini-split installation, heat pump service, and thermostat replacement are services that most HVAC companies offer but forget to enable in LSA. Each unchecked box is an entire category of searches you're missing.
  • No after-hours call handling for emergencies. A furnace dying at 11pm on a Saturday is a $3,000–$6,000 replacement opportunity. If that homeowner calls you and gets voicemail, they're dialing the next contractor in the LSA pack before your message even finishes recording. Use a live answering service or an on-call tech rotation. Emergency HVAC calls are your highest-value leads — treat them that way.
  • Not tracking which job types generate the best return. A $45 lead that becomes a $150 tune-up has very different economics than a $45 lead that becomes a $7,000 AC replacement. Most HVAC companies look at blended cost per lead and call it a day. Break out your numbers by job type — repairs, installations, maintenance — and you'll know exactly where your LSA money is actually working hardest.
  • Letting reviews go dark in the off-season. You get 25 reviews in July, another 15 in January, and then nothing from March through May. Google's algorithm reads that gap as a signal your business may be slowing down. Even during shoulder months, you're running maintenance calls and tune-ups — send a review request after every single one. Four reviews in April beats zero.

Should HVAC Companies Run LSA and Google Ads Together?

Yes — and HVAC is one of the clearest cases for running both channels because your business has two very different customer types. LSA catches the emergency buyer: the homeowner whose AC quit in July or whose furnace stopped working on a freezing night. These callers don't comparison shop. They call whoever Google shows them first with good reviews and a verified badge. That's your LSA sweet spot.

Google Search Ads catch everyone else — and for HVAC, "everyone else" is a huge market. Homeowners researching new system costs before their 15-year-old unit dies. Families comparing SEER ratings and asking whether a heat pump makes sense in their climate. Property managers searching for commercial HVAC maintenance contracts. None of these people trigger LSA because their search isn't urgent enough. But they're your future $6,000–$12,000 installation jobs, and Google Ads is how you get in front of them months before they're ready to buy.

Google Ads also lets you promote seasonal offers that LSA can't touch. Running a $79 spring tune-up special? A $500-off new system installation promo? Google Ads lets you build campaigns around those offers with dedicated landing pages, specific ad copy, and targeted keywords. LSA doesn't support promotions — it's a fixed listing format. The two channels serve fundamentally different purposes, and the HVAC companies that run both consistently see a lower blended cost per booked job than those running either channel alone.

How To Track Your HVAC LSA Results

HVAC jobs range from $150 tune-ups to $12,000 system replacements — so "cost per lead" alone is almost meaningless. Here's how to track what actually matters:

  1. Break out cost per booked job by job type. Track repairs, installations, and maintenance separately. If your LSA is generating mostly $150 tune-up calls at $55/lead, the math is tight. But if those same leads are converting to $5,000 AC replacements at even a 20% rate, the channel is printing money. You can't optimize what you don't segment.
  2. Compare CPL across seasons. Your summer AC leads will cost $60–$90 in most markets. Winter heating leads might run $40–$65. Spring and fall shoulder season leads can drop to $25–$45. Track these seasonally so you can budget accurately next year instead of guessing.
  3. Calculate separate CPBJ for emergency vs. planned work. Emergency calls (no heat, AC died) convert at 70–80% because the homeowner is desperate. Planned work (new system research, maintenance) converts at 30–40%. Blending these together gives you a misleading number. Separate them and you'll see that your emergency lead economics are probably outstanding while your planned work pipeline might need a different approach.
  4. Monitor after-hours call conversion rate. If you're paying for an answering service to cover nights and weekends, track how many of those after-hours calls actually turn into booked jobs. A good after-hours service should be converting at least 40% of emergency calls into next-day appointments. If it's lower, the issue is usually the script or the handoff process, not the leads.
  5. Dispute invalid leads weekly — don't batch monthly. Google has a limited window for disputes. Set a recurring Friday task to review every lead from the past week and dispute wrong numbers, out-of-area calls, and non-HVAC inquiries. Credits from disputes can save you $200–$400/month in wasted spend.

FAQ

Does HVAC LSA cost more in summer vs. winter?

Yes — significantly. Summer AC leads in competitive markets can run $70–$120 per lead because every HVAC company is bidding for the same surge in demand. Winter heating leads tend to be slightly lower at $50–$90, but still elevated compared to shoulder months. Spring and fall typically see the lowest CPL at $25–$50, though lead volume is also lower. Plan your budget around these swings rather than running a flat weekly spend all year.

Are emergency HVAC leads from LSA better than planned maintenance leads?

They convert differently. Emergency leads (no AC, furnace died) close at 70–80% because the homeowner is desperate for same-day service. Planned leads (tune-up, system research) close at 30–40% but can lead to higher-value installations. Both are worth pursuing — the key is tracking them separately so you understand the true return on each type of lead your LSA profile generates.

Can I upsell maintenance plans from LSA repair leads?

Absolutely, and the best HVAC companies do this systematically. When an LSA lead calls for a $300 repair, your tech can offer a $199/year maintenance plan on-site. Over time, maintenance plan customers become your installation pipeline — they call you first when their system needs replacing because they already trust you. LSA pays for the first visit; your maintenance plan pays for the lifetime value.

What HVAC-specific licenses does Google require for LSA verification?

Google requires your state HVAC contractor license and general liability insurance at minimum. Some states require additional certifications like EPA 608 for refrigerant handling. Background checks are required for owners and in some markets for technicians too. Upload everything your state mandates — incomplete documentation is the number one reason HVAC companies get stuck in verification limbo for weeks.

How many HVAC service types should I enable in LSA?

Every single one you can legitimately deliver. Most HVAC companies enable 4–5 obvious services (AC repair, heating repair, installation) and miss another 6–8 they actually offer. Check for duct cleaning, indoor air quality, mini-split service, heat pump repair, thermostat installation, zone system installation, and air handler service. Each enabled service type opens up an entire category of searches you were previously invisible for.

Should I keep LSA running during the HVAC off-season?

Yes — at a reduced budget, not turned off. Shutting off LSA during spring and fall lets your ranking decay and gives competitors who stay active a head start going into peak season. There's still maintenance, tune-up, and installation research happening year-round. A $200–$300/week budget during shoulder months keeps your profile active, builds review momentum, and means you're not starting from scratch when the first heat wave or cold snap hits.
