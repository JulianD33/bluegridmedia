Soft Washing vs. Pressure Washing: What to Say in Your Profile

This distinction matters more than most pressure washers realize, and getting it right in your LSA profile can meaningfully differentiate you from operators who only have one tool in their trailer.

Pressure washing uses high-pressure water, typically 2,000 to 4,000 PSI for residential work and 4,000-plus for commercial applications, to blast dirt off hard surfaces like concrete, brick, and stone. It is fast and effective for driveways, sidewalks, and commercial flatwork.

Soft washing uses low pressure (often 100 PSI or less) combined with surfactants and cleaning detergents to safely clean surfaces that would be damaged or destroyed by high pressure. Vinyl siding, stucco, wood fences, roofs, and painted wood all require soft washing. High-pressure washing on a composition shingle roof can strip the granules off, void the warranty, and create water infiltration issues. Homeowners who have done even a little research know this, and they will ask about it before they book.

Why does this matter for your LSA profile? Because "soft wash" is a real search term homeowners use when they are worried about their siding or roof. If you offer soft washing and your profile description only mentions pressure washing, you are invisible for those searches. Mention it explicitly: "soft wash siding," "low-pressure roof cleaning," and "soft wash house washing" are all descriptive terms that set you apart from operators who are still spraying everything at max pressure.

If you offer soft washing, call it out clearly. If you do not, at minimum be accurate about what services you can safely deliver. The operators who get the best LSA results in this trade are the ones who are specific, not generic.

The Roof Cleaning Opportunity Hiding in Plain Sight

Roof soft washing deserves its own section because it is genuinely one of the most underutilized job types in the entire pressure washing LSA category. The black streaks on asphalt shingles that homeowners see across their neighborhood every summer are caused by a roof algae called Gloeocapsa magma. The treatment is low-pressure washing with an appropriate biocide solution. Average job: 00 to 00 for a standard residential roof, more for two-story homes and larger footprints.

Most pressure washing operators either do not offer roof cleaning because they do not have the right equipment setup, or they offer it but have not enabled the job type in LSA. The result: a homeowner searching "roof cleaning near me" in your market often finds almost nobody in the LSA pack. If you offer this service and have it enabled, you could be the only Google Verified option they see. That is not a metaphor. That is literally the situation in a large number of U.S. suburban markets right now.

If you do not currently offer roof washing but want to add it, the startup cost is modest: a 12-volt soft wash pump system, appropriate nozzles, and cleaning chemistry. The service pays for the equipment quickly at 00 to 00 per job. And when you are the only LSA provider in your area offering roof cleaning, you set the price.

Seasonality Strategy: When to Spend, When to Pull Back

Pressure washing is one of the more seasonal trades on LSA. Spring is the peak of peaks. Summer holds strong. Fall is decent in many markets. Winter in cold climates is nearly dead. The mistake most operators make is treating LSA like a faucet they turn on in March and turn off in November. That is expensive and it costs you ranking position when you come back in spring.

The right strategy is to think in terms of intensity levels, not on/off:

  • March through May (peak ramp): Increase budget aggressively heading into spring. Homeowners are making exterior cleaning decisions for the year.   • June through August (full season): Run at full budget. Demand is high, leads convert quickly, and same-week or next-day scheduling is realistic. Answer every call. Your response speed during this window directly impacts your LSA ranking for the rest of the year.
  • September through October (moderate pullback): Reduce budget by 30 to 40% but stay visible. Fall cleanup demand is real in many markets, especially for gutter cleaning and preparing decks before winter. Customers who hire you in October for gutter cleaning often call you back in spring.
  • November through February in cold markets (keep-alive mode): Pull back to a minimal budget. The goal is not lead volume, it is keeping your ranking from decaying entirely. An account that goes dark for three months comes back weak in spring. Stay barely active, collect any reviews from late-season jobs, and you will be in much better shape when March hits.
  • Year-round markets (Florida, Texas, Arizona, Southern California): None of this seasonal adjustment applies. Pressure washing demand is consistent enough that full budget year-round is defensible. Your only budget consideration is your own crew capacity, not the season.

The Upsell Path That Turns $150 Jobs Into $500 Days

Here is one of the most underappreciated financial advantages of pressure washing: the upsell path is almost always sitting right in front of you when you show up on site. A customer calls for a driveway cleaning. You show up and you can see the house siding is grimy, the fence is green with algae, and the deck looks like it has not been touched in three years. If you do not mention it, you drive away with $150. If you mention it naturally, you might drive away with $480 and a customer who rebooks every year.

A realistic pressure washing upsell sequence looks like this:

  1. Anchor job: Driveway cleaning ($120 to $200). Customer found you on LSA specifically for this service.
  2. First add-on: House washing, the siding you can see from the driveway ($150 to $300 depending on home size). Easy to propose while you are already set up and the hose is running.
  3. Second add-on: Fence washing ($50 to $100). Takes maybe 15 to 20 extra minutes on equipment you already have running.
  4. Third add-on: Deck cleaning ($75 to $150). If there is a deck visible, it almost always needs attention after a winter.
  5. Future booking: Roof soft washing ($300 to $500, schedule for next visit). Not always same-day, but plant the seed before you leave and follow up the next week.

The average ticket on a multi-surface job goes from $150 to $450 to $600 quickly once you build this conversation into your standard on-site interaction. You do not need to be pushy. "While I am here, did you want me to hit the siding too? I am already set up and it would take me another 20 minutes" is enough. Most homeowners say yes when the question is that easy.

This matters for LSA because your budget efficiency improves dramatically when your average job value goes up. At $30 CPL and an average ticket of $500 instead of $150, you are spending 6% of revenue on acquisition instead of 20%. That is the difference between running a tight operation and a genuinely profitable one.

Review Strategy (and How to Get Photos in Reviews)

Pressure washing is one of the most visually satisfying trades in existence. A black, algae-covered driveway turning to clean white concrete is the kind of transformation that gets shared, screenshot, and shown to neighbors at the weekend cookout. This is a massive advantage for your review strategy that most operators do not fully exploit.

Getting Reviews After Every Job

Send a text message after every completed job. Keep it short: "Hi [Name], thanks for choosing us today. If you are happy with how everything looks, we would really appreciate a quick Google review. Here is the link: [direct GBP review link]." That is it. A two-sentence text sent the afternoon after job completion is enough.

A complete system for generating reviews, maintaining review velocity, and handling negative reviews is in our LSA review strategy guide. The short version: consistency beats volume bursts. Ten reviews per month every month for six months beats sixty reviews in one month followed by silence. Google’s algorithm rewards recency and consistency, not historical peak counts.

Getting Photos in Reviews

Here is something most pressure washers never think to ask: you can encourage customers to include before-and-after photos in their Google review. When you send the review request text, add one sentence: "If you snapped a before/after photo, feel free to include it in the review. It helps other homeowners see the difference." A meaningful percentage will do exactly that, and it costs you nothing.

Reviews with before-and-after photos are extraordinarily persuasive. A potential customer scrolling your reviews and seeing a picture of a black driveway next to a gleaming clean one is seeing the best sales pitch you could ever produce. Reviews mentioning specific surfaces like concrete, vinyl siding, and roof cleaning also help with job-type-specific ranking in LSA. The more specific the language in your reviews, the better for your visibility in category-specific searches.

For your own profile photos: shoot before-and-after on every job. Driveways, siding, decks, fences. Your phone in good daylight is sufficient. Make it part of your job start process to take a "before" photo and your job close-out process to take an "after" photo. After six months you will have more compelling visual content than most pressure washing competitors ever accumulate.

Pressure Washing LSA Economics: CPL, Booking Rate, Cost Per Job

Pressure washing has some of the best LSA unit economics of any trade category. Here is the math:

  • Average CPL (cost per lead): $20 to $45 in suburban markets, $35 to $60 in major metros. This is genuinely low compared to HVAC ($60 to $120-plus) or water damage restoration ($80 to $150-plus).
  • Answer rate target: 75% to 85%. These are quick, day-of or next-day jobs. Customers are not going to wait long to hear back. Answer or call back within 30 minutes.
  • Booking rate on answered calls: 55% to 70%. Pressure washing is a commodity purchase for most homeowners. Price and availability drive the decision. If you answer quickly and have availability that week, you book a high percentage.
  • Cost per booked job at baseline: $30 CPL divided by (0.75 multiplied by 0.60) equals approximately $67.
  • Average job value (single service): $150 to $250.
  • Average job value (multi-surface upsell): $400 to $600.

At a $67 cost per booked job against a $200 single-service job, you are spending 33% of revenue on acquisition. That is why the upsell conversation matters so much. At a $500 average ticket, your acquisition cost drops to 13% of revenue. At $600, you are at 11%. The margin transformation is entirely in your hands based on how you run on-site conversations.

Running two to four jobs per day in peak season at $300 to $500 average each means $600 to $2,000 in daily revenue. At a $67 cost per booked job with a crew running four jobs, you are spending $268 on acquisition and generating between $1,200 and $2,000 in revenue. Those are solid numbers for a small operation, and they improve as your reviews accumulate and your ranking strengthens over time.

Common Mistakes Pressure Washers Make on LSA

  • Not setting up LSA at all. Genuinely the most common situation. Most pressure washing operators in most markets are not on LSA. If you set it up today, complete the verification, and get your first ten reviews, you will likely be the best-ranked pressure washing LSA in your service area within 60 to 90 days. That is not hyperbole. It is the current state of the market in most areas.
  • Only enabling two or three job types. Driveway cleaning and house washing are obvious. Roof cleaning, gutter cleaning, deck cleaning, commercial building washing, and fence washing all generate independent search volume. Each unchecked job type box is a category of searches you are completely invisible for. Enable everything you can legitimately and safely deliver.
  • Not mentioning soft washing anywhere. "Pressure washing" is the generic category. "Soft wash house washing," "low-pressure roof cleaning," and "soft wash vinyl siding" are the specific terms that informed customers search. Including these in your profile description and business summary captures searches that generic profiles miss entirely.
  • Turning off LSA entirely in November. Your ranking decays when your account goes dark. Competitors who stay active at a reduced budget through winter come into spring ranked above you. Keep a minimal budget running. Even $50 to $75 per week in the off-season is enough to maintain your position without generating leads you cannot serve.
  • Slow call response. Pressure washing customers have low patience. They searched, they called, they want to know if you can come this week. If you do not pick up or call back within 30 minutes, they have already called someone else. Google also tracks your call response rate and uses it as a ranking signal. Fast response is simultaneously good customer service and good SEO. For a full rundown on what hurts your ranking, see our LSA ranking factors guide.
  • Ignoring the environmental compliance angle in regulated markets. Some municipalities restrict certain cleaning detergents from entering storm drains. Commercial wash jobs in some areas require reclaim systems to capture runoff. If you are in a regulated market and you have done the compliance work, mentioning it in your profile is a genuine differentiator. It signals to commercial customers that you operate professionally.
  • No before-and-after photos in the profile. This trade photographs beautifully. A clean driveway next to a dirty one is worth a thousand words in your listing. If you have no photos in your profile, competitors with photos will beat you on conversions even if your reviews are similar. Add photos before you launch. Add more photos as you go.

Yes. Google LSA supports pressure washing and exterior cleaning as an eligible category. The verification process requires proof of general liability insurance and a background check on business owners. Most states do not have a specific pressure washing license, though some municipalities have environmental regulations around chemical runoff that may apply to your operation. What is the difference between pressure washing and soft washing on LSA?

Pressure washing uses high-pressure water, typically 2,000 to 4,000 PSI, to clean hard surfaces like concrete and brick. Soft washing uses low pressure combined with cleaning detergents to safely clean delicate surfaces like vinyl siding, stucco, roofs, and wood fences that would be damaged by high pressure. On your LSA profile, being clear about which method you use for which surfaces helps you capture customers who are specifically searching for soft washing because they know high pressure can damage their roof or siding. Operators who offer soft washing and mention it in their profile consistently convert more leads than operators who only list "pressure washing" as their service.

Should pressure washers pause LSA in winter?

In cold-weather markets, pulling back budget heavily November through February makes sense because residential demand genuinely drops. However, fully pausing causes your ranking to decay.
